Slash

Slashes are used to create dynamic overlaps between images and content. They are most commonly used as solid blocks of color paired with text or an image, or with an image placed inside. Use them for introductory pages such as cover pages, title slides, heros, or to draw attention to areas of text such as pull quotes.

Degree of Angle

There are two standard degrees of the angle slash, vertical and horizontal.

Vertical Slash

75°

Used on designs with a landscape orientation. Its slash degree should be 75°, regardless of its container. Below are guidelines for use and examples.




Horizontal Slash

15°

Used on designs with a portrait orientation. Its slash degree should be 15°, regardless of its container.

Direction and Position

The standard direction of the slash is angled towards the upper-right side of the page, and the shape should always touch three edges of the page. However, it is acceptable to position it to the left as long as it is relative to the content. Legibility is our primary goal– always allow the content of the page to determine its position.

Don't



The slash should never overpower or cramp the text. Proper margins should be given to any text the slash is paired with, and the slash should never exceed more than half of the total design.

Dash

Dashes are placed below slide titles to create a visual break between header to body, or to separate sections of text. These dashes add visual balance and hierarchy to large areas of text. Below are two specifications to keep in mind when using the dash.

Vertical Spacing

The dash should always be closer in proximity to the title than its subtext. When in doubt, stick to the rule of thirds–there should always be more space after the dash.

Don't


Length

Dashes are most often paired with titles. They can also be used as visual breaks. Determining the length of each dash depends on the font size of its title. Strokes also vary by size, but are typically 2-3px.

Dashes should be around 2-3 letters in length. Always round its length to the nearest multiple of five pixels.
